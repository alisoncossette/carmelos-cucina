MakerMods Hackathon · 2026-05-10

Carmelo's Cucina

A safety-first kitchen companion for an elderly user. Built on a dual-arm SO-101, orchestrated by a vision-language model, designed around the assumption that policies will fail.

01
Who we built for

Carmelo wants to keep making his own toast.

Independent living for elderly users isn't about removing them from their kitchen — it's about handling the few steps that have become hard or unsafe, while leaving everything else to them.

A robot that does the whole task is the wrong product. A robot that helps with the parts Carmelo can't easily do — and asks for help on the rest — is the right one.

02
The hard part

Robots fail. The question is how.

~80%
ACT reliability we observed on this task. Solid — but not 100%.
jumpy
SmolVLA / pi behavior on tight pick-and-place. Language-conditioned VLAs aren't muscle memory.
partial
Real failure we hit: the toaster-on policy doesn't always fully depress the lever.

The choice isn't autonomy vs nothing. It's fragile autonomy that fails badly versus mostly-autonomy that fails courteously.

03
Thesis

Robots that ask for help
instead of failing badly.

01

Detect

A VLM watches both cameras at 1–2 Hz and reports task state, safety state, and presence. A debounced FSM filters out hallucinations.

02

Communicate

The arm itself waves "no" via runmotion.ai. The agent narrates intent and confirms before acting. Carmelo is never surprised.

03

Defer

If a skill fails twice, the robot pauses, asks Carmelo to help with that step, and resumes from the next state once acknowledged.

04
Architecture

A supervisor in the loop, not in the controller.

VLM decides which skill to run; ACT handles execution. Either side swaps without retraining the other.

wrist cam + env cam → VLM @ 1.5 Hz → JSON state → FSM (debounced ×3) → policy dispatch
SafetyMonitor · GestureClient (runmotion.ai) · AgentClient

The VLM emits one JSON per tick covering both task progress (bread_in_toaster, lever_down, toast_popped) and safety signals (human_hand_visible, workspace_clear, confidence). The FSM consumes both streams.

05
Safety, three layers deep

The runtime policies don't know about safety. We do.

Preflight gate

Refuses to dispatch when the world isn't safe.

halt hand in workspace halt hot toaster + bread insert halt press without bread wait low VLM confidence

Runtime watchdog

Kills in-flight skills the moment something goes wrong.

e-stop hand mid-skill e-stop lever down mid-insert e-stop heartbeat stale halt VLM blind for N ticks

Fail-safe defaults

When the VLM emits invalid JSON, we assume the worst.

human_hand_visible: true
workspace_clear: false
confidence: low

Halt rather than act on garbage.

06
No speaker. No screen.

The arm itself talks back.

Carmelo's kitchen has no audio output. So when the robot has to pause, it communicates through motion — the same arm that was reaching for bread now waves "no" at the user, then settles back to home pose.

Embodied feedback. Universal. No language barrier, no learning curve, no app.

wave_no side-to-side wrist wave
return_home smooth move to safe pose
thumbs_up success — toast popped
ack nod — "I see you"

Powered by runmotion.ai for choreographed gesture playback.

07
The proof: a real failure

Policy B sometimes doesn't fully depress the lever.

Recorded today by the team. This is exactly the failure mode the orchestrator is designed for.

What happens, end to end

1. FSM is in PRESSING. Dispatcher launches the toaster-on policy.

2. Skill ends. VLM still reports lever_down: false.

3. FSM stays in PRESSING, dispatches a second attempt.

4. Second attempt ends. Lever still up. Attempt counter hits the cap.

5. Arm waves wave_no, returns to return_home.

6. Agent: "I'm having trouble pressing the lever down. Could you help me with that part?"

7. Carmelo presses the lever. Acknowledges. Robot resets, advances to TOASTING, waits for the pop.

The robot did 90% of the task and asked for help on the 10% it couldn't do — instead of failing silently or burning Carmelo's hand.

08
Why this matters for MakerMods

Closing the safety gap they explicitly leave open.

"AI automations are for convenience only, not safety or security-critical use cases."
— makermods.ai/modblocks

MakerMods makes AI robotics accessible. They explicitly carve out safety-critical use as out of scope. Carmelo's Cucina is the layer that closes that gap — preflight gates, runtime watchdogs, embodied feedback, and graceful handoff — turning a convenience-grade stack into one safe enough to deploy around a vulnerable user.

The orchestrator is hardware-agnostic. Plug in any USB-C ModBlock for the I/O surface, any LeRobot policy for the muscle memory.

Stack
